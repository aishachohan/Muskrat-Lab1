Muskrat Hut Surveying in the Peace Athabasca Delta


Muskrats,Ondatra zibethicus, are a keystone species in the Peace-Athabasca Delta (PAD). Though their native range spans much of North America, these semi-aquatic mammals are particularly important to the ecosystem and the people of the Peace-Athabasca Delta. Historically, the PAD marshes and wetlands provide habitat for tens of thousands of muskrats. In turn, muskrats assured food for predators and people alike. In the fur trade era, local Indigenous trappers sold thousands of muskrat furs in a season. Muskrats not only provided food and fur to be consumed locally, they were the basis of an economy and a way of life. One land user described muskrats as “the bank”, a reliable source of income to buy fuel for skidoos or food for a family
In 2011, in response to concerns regarding muskrat population dynamics, Parks Canada staff met with Fort Chip community members through the Peace-Athabasca Delta Ecological Monitoring Program (PADEMP), a collaboration of the Mikisew Cree First Nation (MCFN), the Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation (ACFN), and Fort Chip Métis Association (FCMA). From that workshop, a long-term monitoring effort known as the PAD Muskrat Survey was born. Every winter field crews composed of Indigenous land users and Parks Canada staff studied muskrat abundance from house counts. The survey method was based upon earlier surveys in the 1970s, 1990s, and 2000s where the number of houses was counted in certain study basins. This allowed new data collected from 2011-2016 to be combined with older surveys in order to track the decrease in muskrat densities over a longer period of time.
